## Nickel allergy, a major jewelry issue

Nickel allergy affects between 10 and 15 percent of the European adult population according to data published by the French Society of Dermatology in 2024, with a clear female predominance, around 17 percent of women versus 3 percent of men. **Ear piercing** is one of the main triggers of this sensitization, since piercing creates an open wound in direct contact with the metal for several weeks.

The European REACH directive has limited nickel release since 2001 to 0.2 microgram per square centimeter per week for jewelry in prolonged contact with skin, and to 0.5 microgram for pieces inserted in a pierced ear during healing. Shops selling in Europe must therefore comply with this threshold, but technical compliance does not systematically protect already sensitized skin. This is where the **ASTM F136 standard** becomes meaningful: it guarantees implant grade titanium with nickel content below 0.05 percent and non-releasable, ten times stricter than REACH.

### Why check the product sheet before buying

For an allergic buyer, three pieces of information must appear in black and white on the product sheet before any purchase:

- The exact material standard, ideally ASTM F136 for titanium or a clear karat marking for solid gold
- The maximum nickel percentage or the explicit mention "no releasable nickel"
- REACH compliance or medical device certification for healing piercings

The absence of any of these three mentions should raise concern, especially on pieces sold as "surgical steel" without grade detail. Low-grade 316L can contain up to 14 percent nickel according to the French National Institute for Research and Safety.

## How to choose your shop in case of nickel allergy

The selection method rests on three simple steps applicable to any online shop, regardless of its notoriety.

First, read the product sheet before adding to cart and verify explicit mention of the material standard. Absence of the ASTM F136 standard on titanium or of a karat marking on solid gold is an immediate elimination signal, no exception. Then, cross this information with the care sheet and placement recommendations, which must mention the REACH directive or medical device certification for healing pieces. Finally, check the return policy for unused piercings, since a delayed reaction after placement may require urgent material change.

### Mistakes to avoid

1. Buying a "surgical steel" piercing without exact grade mention, since low-grade 316L can release nickel according to the French INRS
2. Confusing **gold plating** and solid gold, plating wears off in a few months and exposes the underlying metal layer often based on copper and nickel
3. Reusing an old piercing as healing jewelry after a new piercing, the risk of infection and reaction is multiplied
4. Relying on the "hypoallergenic" mention alone, this term is not regulated in jewelry in Europe and has no enforceable legal value

<summary>Should I remove a piercing in case of redness after placement?</summary>

Slight redness and mild swelling in the first days are normal and do not justify removal. In this case, consult a dermatologist before any removal, since rushed removal can trap infection under the skin. If allergy is confirmed, switch to ASTM F136 titanium or solid 14 karat gold without nickel.
