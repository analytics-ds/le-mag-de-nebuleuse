## Why buy single ear piercings for a curated ear

The **curated ear** refers to the intentional arrangement of several piercings and pieces of jewelry on the same ear, designed as a coherent set rather than isolated placements. The concept was popularized and trademarked as Curated Ear by jeweler Maria Tash, before spreading widely across feminine jewelry.

Building a curated ear requires buying jewelry **individually** rather than in pairs. A lobe, helix, tragus, conch or daith each receives a single piece, chosen for its size, material and style. Piece-by-piece sale therefore becomes the norm on specialized sites, unlike classic earrings sold in twos.

The material in contact with the skin directly conditions the success of a curated ear, since most placements sit on cartilage, a thin area that is slow to heal. The **Association of Professional Piercers** recommends for any healing piercing ASTM F136 implant-grade titanium, solid gold of at least 14 carats without nickel, or niobium. Low-grade gold plating and 925 silver may contain nickel, the leading contact allergen according to French public health data published in 2024.

The choice of site therefore depends first on the material offered, then on pricing position and catalog depth. Before any purchase, it helps to know the placements and their healing times, a point detailed in the [ear piercing guide](/en/blog/ear-piercing-guide/) that reviews each area of the ear.

### Site selection criteria

- Genuine individual sale rather than forced pairs
- Material in contact with the piercing (implant-grade titanium, solid gold, gold plating, silver)
- Catalog depth by placement (lobe, helix, tragus, conch, daith)
- Pricing position and budget of a full arrangement
- Shipping and return conditions in metropolitan France

## Practical tips for building a curated ear

Building a **curated ear** rests on a few checkpoints. The first is material consistency: mixing titanium and solid gold stays risk-free, while low-grade gold plating wears faster with contact and perspiration. The second is the order of placement, since cartilage piercings heal slowly and are composed progressively, placement after placement. The third is the size of the jewelry, chosen by placement to avoid compression points on the cartilage.

| Placement | Ear area | Average healing | Recommended material |
|-----------|----------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Lobe | Lower soft part | 6 to 8 weeks | Titanium, solid gold |
| Helix | Upper cartilage | 6 to 12 months | Implant-grade titanium |
| Tragus | Cartilage in front of canal | 4 to 8 months | Implant-grade titanium |
| Conch | Central cartilage | 6 to 12 months | Implant-grade titanium |
| Daith | Inner cartilage fold | 6 to 12 months | Implant-grade titanium |

A curated ear is built over time, adding pieces as healing progresses. Jewelry that does not require a piercing, such as [ear cuffs](/en/blog/where-to-buy-ear-cuffs/), helps complete the arrangement without a new placement and test a spot before piercing it.

### Mistakes to avoid

1. Buying a pair when a single piercing is enough, when individual sale is the norm on specialized sites
2. Placing gold-plated or low-grade silver jewelry on a fresh piercing, the first factor of allergic reaction
3. Composing the whole ear at once, without respecting the healing times specific to each placement
4. Overlooking the titanium standard, since only ASTM F136 implant grade guarantees safety on cartilage
5. Stacking jewelry too close together, which creates compression points and can cause chondritis
